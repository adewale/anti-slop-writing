Do not break the tie on taste. "I like the cadence" versus "it hides the relation" is not actually a disagreement about preference; it is a disagreement that the current process has no evidence standard to resolve. The fix is to make grading cite the text.

Notice the two graders may both be right about different things. A rewrite can read well and still bury the logical relation between two clauses, which is precisely the failure the skill warns about: rhythm standing in for a relation that was never named. So the question is not "whose ear is better," it is "show me the spot." Require each grader to quote the specific before/after span they are judging and say what it does:

- The cadence grader quotes the line and names what makes it work (varied rhythm, a concrete carrier, a memorable close).
- The relation grader quotes the same line and shows the missing connective: which idea is supposed to modify which, and where a reader would have to guess the cause, contrast, or dependency that the parataxis left implicit.

With both quotes on the table, the disagreement usually resolves on the evidence. If the relation is recoverable from the surrounding sentences, the cadence grader wins and the rhythm was earned. If a reader has to supply the "because" or "although" themselves, the relation grader wins and the line needs subordination, regardless of how good it sounds. The rubric already has the test for this (the flow-by-relation and staccato-contrast checks); the grading process just was not forcing graders to apply it to a quoted span.

What the eval process should require, going forward:

1. Evidence-backed grading. Every score carries a quote from the rewrite or the source and a one-line reason tied to a named rubric criterion. No bare verdicts.
2. A concrete before/after for any "better/worse" claim. You cannot grade an improvement without both states visible.
3. A tie-break path. When evidence-backed graders still split, escalate to a human review of that one case rather than averaging two opinions into a number that means nothing.
4. Feedback into the suite. A recurring disagreement is a signal the rubric is ambiguous on this exact axis. Tighten the criterion (define when cadence overrides relation and when it does not) and add this case to the suite as a worked example, so the next occurrence is adjudicated by precedent instead of re-argued.

The reusable structure: judge disagreement is diagnostic, not noise. It points at the place where the rubric is underspecified. Resolve the instance with quoted evidence and a human tie-break, then close the gap by sharpening the rubric and capturing the case, so the ambiguity does not recur.
